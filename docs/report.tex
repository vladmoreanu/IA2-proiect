\begin{abstract}
Image restoration remains a fundamental challenge in computer vision, with
applications ranging from medical imaging to computational photography. This work
investigates the effectiveness of convolutional neural network-based restoration
on synthetically degraded images, evaluating performance across a range of
controlled blur and noise conditions. A selection of four restoration models are
surveyed, spanning convolutional, transformer-based, and generative
architectures. A residual convolutional model is trained and evaluated
on a dataset of patch-based samples subject to varying degrees of Gaussian blur
and additive Gaussian noise, under constrained hardware conditions. Results are
discussed in the context of the broader restoration landscape, highlighting the
trade-offs between architectural complexity and restoration performance.
\end{abstract}

The landscape of image restoration and synthesis has advanced rapidly, moving from highly optimized
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to highly efficient Vision Transformers and diffusion models.
Each state-of-the-art (SOTA) model introduces distinct architectural innovations to overcome the
trade-offs between computational cost and restoration accuracy

\subsection{Convolutional Neural Networks}

DnCNN (Denoising Convolutional Neural Network) introduces a residual learning strategy combined with
batch normalization. Instead of predicting the clean image directly, the model learns to predict the
residual noise. This speeds up training, stabilizes the network and allows a single model (DnCNN-B)
to handle blind gaussian denoising. \cite{beyond_a_gaussian}

This strategy allowed DnCNN-B to outperform AR-CNN, a specialized model, by 0.3 dB in PSNR.

FFDNet brings a tunable noise level map as a direct input, allowing a single network to flexibly
handle complex, non-uniform noise. Additionally, it processes the image in a downsampled sub-image
space, which vastly improves inference speed and expands the receptive field without losing modeling
capacity. \cite{FFDNet}

This model can keep a performance of 26-27 dB PSNR across a vast noise level range, while being
significantly faster then DnCNN.

\subsection{High-Efficiency and Adversarial Architectures}

DeblurGAN approaches blind motion deblurring as an image-to-image translation task using Conditional
Generative Adversarial Networks (cGANs). This introduces a multi-component loss function, combining
WGAN-GP and perceptual loss, to generate sharp and realistic textures rather than the overly smooth
results found in basic CNNs. It's efficient architercture makes it 5 times faster than other models,
making it a good choice for downstream object detectuin tasks. On the GoPro dataset, it achieved a
PSNR of 29.7 dB. \cite{Deblurgan}

MIMO-UNet brings the re-engineering of the coarse-to-fine strategy into a single U-Net. It uses a
Multi-Input Single Encoder (MISE) to process multi-scale blurry images simultaneously, and a
Multi-Output Single Decoder (MOSD) to output multiple deblurred images, achieving SOTA accuracy
while running over 4 times faster than previous complex models. \cite{Rethinking}

The largest model (MIMO-Unet++) has a PSNR of 32.68 dB on the GoPro dataset, with a processing time
of 0.004 seconds.

\subsection{Vision Transformers}

SwinIR, based on the Swin transformer, replaces traditional convolution layers with Residual Swin
Transformer Blocks (RSTB). This has the advantage of local attention processing and shifted-window
mechanisms for modeling long range dependencies. The model. For the super-resolution task it has
achieved a PSNR of 32.7 dB, while having significantly less parameters than models with the same
results. \cite{Swinir}

Uformer brings transformer capabilities into a classic U-shaped arhitecture. It's contribution is
the Locally-enhaced Window (LeWin) Transformer block, which calculates self-attention only within
non-overlapping windows to reduce computational complexity. It also introduces a learnable
multi-scale restoration modulator to handle varied image degradations. \cite{Uformer}

On image denoising, Uformer achieved 39.9 dB in PSNR, outperforming heavier models. For debluring,
on the GoPro dataset, the model obtained a result of 33 dB 

Restormer brings Multi-Dconv Head Transposed Attention (MDTA), which calculates self-attention
across the feature (channel) dimension rather than the spatial dimension, bringing the
self-attention complexity down to a linear scale, which is quadratic for normal transformers. It
also introduces a Gated-Dconv Feed-Forward Network (GDFN) to act as a filter for less important
features. For the task of denoising, it has achived a PSNR of 40 dB, and 33 dB for deblurring.
\cite{Restormer}

\subsection{Generative Synthesis}

While diffusion models are powerful, calculating them directly in pixel space is incredibly slow and
expensive. Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) bring the innovation of decoupling perceptual and semantic
compression. By first training an autoencoder to compress images into a lower-dimensional latent
space, the diffusion process can occur much more efficiently. This allows the model to strike a
near-optimal balance between complexity reduction and detail preservation. Furthermore, LDMs
introduce cross-attention layers into the diffusion UNet, making them incredibly flexible for
multi-modal conditioning. \cite{High-Resolution_image}

\section{Methods and Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

This section details the experimental methodology employed to evaluate
denoising performance across varying degradation conditions. We first describe
the construction of the Flickr2K-derived dataset variants, outlining the
preprocessing pipeline and the rationale behind the chosen degradation
parameters. We then present the architectures evaluated in this study—DnCNN,
ResUNet, Uformer, and Reformer—along with their respective training
configurations, providing the necessary context for interpreting the
comparative results discussed in later sections.

The Flickr2K dataset~\cite{Lim_2017_CVPR_Workshops}, comprising approximately 2,600 high-resolution
images, was used as the primary data source. All images were resized and center-cropped to a uniform
resolution of $1024 \times 1024$ pixels prior to preprocessing. Each image then underwent a
three-stage degradation pipeline: Gaussian blur with a fixed kernel size of 5, additive Gaussian
noise, and finally tiling into non-overlapping $128 \times 128$ patches, yielding a substantial
number of training samples per image.
The choice of patch size was motivated by the limited hardware available for training,
as smaller patches reduce memory overhead while still providing sufficient context for
local feature extraction. 
To evaluate model robustness across a range of degradation conditions, seven dataset
variants were constructed by varying the blur and noise parameters. Three variants
isolate the effect of blur by applying Gaussian blur at $\sigma \in \{5, 10, 15\}$
with no additive noise. A further three variants isolate noise by applying additive
Gaussian noise at $\sigma \in \{15, 20, 25\}$ with no blur. A final mixed-degradation
variant combines both blur ($\sigma_b = 10$) and noise ($\sigma_n = 15$), representing
a more realistic compound degradation scenario. A summary of all dataset configurations
is provided in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats}, along with the average PSNR and MSE metrics for each
variant of the degradation conditions.


\subsection{DnCNN}

The DnCNN architecture follows the design of~\cite{beyond_a_gaussian},
consisting of 17 convolutional layers with $3 \times 3$ filters and 64 feature
maps throughout. The first layer is a plain convolution followed by ReLU
activation, the intermediate 15 layers each combine convolution, batch
normalisation, and ReLU, and the final layer is a convolution that produces the
estimated noise residual. The clean image is recovered via residual learning,
subtracting the predicted residual directly from the input, which simplifies the
learning target and accelerates convergence.

Experiments were conducted using a grouped 5-fold cross-validation strategy, applied
to the first 100 images of each dataset variant due to hardware constraints. Folds
were generated using \texttt{GroupKFold} from scikit-learn at the patch level,
resulting in an 80/20 train-validation split per fold. Each fold was trained with a
batch size of 16 patches using the Adam optimiser with a learning rate of
$1 \times 10^{-3}$ and a mean squared error loss function over 10 epochs, with
training curves for all folds provided in the Appendices. Convergence was generally
consistent across epochs, with at most one outlier epoch per fold exhibiting
anomalous loss behaviour. Total training time amounted to approximately 10 hours
across all 35 folds (5 folds $\times$ 7 variants), at roughly 1 hour 45 minutes
per variant, on a system equipped with an NVIDIA GTX 980 (4\,GiB VRAM),
an Intel Core i5-6600 CPU, and 16\,GiB of system RAM.

\subsection{ResUNet}

\begin{figure*}[tb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{slides/Res_U-net.drawio.png}
  \caption{The ResUNet architecture.}
  \label{fig:resunet_arch}
\end{figure*}

The ResUNet architecture is a hybrid deep learning model that integrates the U-Net arhitecture
with the optimization benefits of residual learning and residual blocks.
Originally designed for semantic segmentation (U-Net),
this architecture has proven highly effective for pixel-level image-to-image translation tasks,
including joint image denoising and deblurring.
The classic U-Net framework relies on a symmetric encoder path to capture global contextual information
and an expanding decoder path to enable precise spatial reconstruction.
ResUNet enhances this by replacing standard sequential convolutional layers within the encoder and decoder blocks with residual blocks.
This modification addresses the vanishing gradient problem inherent in very deep neural networks,
enabling stable training of deeper architectures and accelerating overall convergence.

The output $H(x)$ of a residual unit is defined as:

$$
H(x) = F(x) + x
$$

where $x$ represents the input tensor to the block,
and $F(x)$ denotes the residual mapping learned.
Instead of forcing the network parameters to fit an entirely new underlying mapping,
the convolutional layers are only required to learn the residual difference $F(x) = H(x) - x$.

The decoder mirrors the encoder's structure,
progressively restoring the spatial dimensions of the feature maps back to the original input resolution.
Upsampling is performed at each decoder stage using transposed convolutions or bilinear interpolation.
Skip connections copy the high-resolution feature maps from the encoder stages
and concatenate them directly with the corresponding upsampled features in the decoder.
By concatenating these raw, high-resolution features directly back to the decoding layers,
the network bypasses the deep bottlenecks and preserves fine-grained structural integrity,
preventing the over-smoothing common in standard autoencoder structures.

\subsection{Uformer}

Uformer follows the architecture described in its original formulation,
employing a U-shaped hierarchical encoder-decoder built from Locally-enhanced
Window (LeWin) Transformer blocks. The model operates on 128-by-128 input
patches with an initial embedding dimension of 32, using a window size of 8 for
the windowed self-attention mechanism. Token projection is performed via a
linear layer, and the token-wise MLP uses the locally-enhanced feed-forward
(LeFF) variant to better capture local context within the transformer blocks.
The encoder-decoder follows a depth configuration of [1, 2, 8, 8, 2, 8, 8, 2,
1] across its stages, and a modulator is incorporated to enhance the network's
capacity to handle spatially-varying degradations. The input channel dimension
is set to 3, corresponding to RGB images.

Weights pre-trained on the GoPro dataset, a benchmark commonly used for
motion deblurring tasks, were used to initialise the model. Evaluation was
performed on the 20\% validation split of the 7th variant of the Flickr2K
dataset, consistent with the held-out portion used for the other models.


\subsection{Reformer}

Restormer follows the architecture described in its original formulation,
employing a U-shaped encoder-decoder built from transposed self-attention and
gated-dconv feed-forward transformer blocks. The model takes 3-channel RGB
input and output, with an initial feature dimension of 48 that doubles at each
stage of the encoder. The encoder-decoder follows a block configuration of [4,
6, 6, 8] across its four stages, with attention head counts of [1, 2, 4, 8]
increasing correspondingly with depth, and a further 4 refinement blocks are
applied at the output stage to enhance feature detail. The feed-forward
expansion factor is set to 2.66, no bias terms are used in the convolutional
and normalisation layers, and layer normalisation is applied with a learnable
bias term. The dual-pixel task setting is disabled, as this configuration is
not specific to dual-pixel defocus deblurring.

Pretrained weights trained on the SIDD dataset, a benchmark widely used for
real-world image denoising, were used to initialise the model. Evaluation was
performed on the 20\% validation split of the 7th variant of the Flickr2K
dataset, consistent with the held-out portion used for the other models.

To evaluate the effectiveness of deep learning approaches for this task, we
conduct a series of experiments involving four models: DnCNN~\cite{beyond_a_gaussian},
ResUNet, Uformer~\cite{Uformer}, and Restormer~\cite{Restormer}.
DnCNN and ResUNet are trained and evaluated through multiple experimental
configurations on the Flickr2K dataset, allowing for a
systematic comparison of training strategies and architectural choices. In
contrast, Uformer and Restormer, being large-scale transformer-based
architectures with substantial pre-trained weights, are evaluated solely on the
Flickr2K validation subset to assess their generalisation capability in a
zero-shot setting. Together, these four models span a range of architectural
paradigms—from convolutional networks to attention-based transformers—providing
a comprehensive benchmark for assessing performance across different levels of
model complexity and training supervision.

The DnCNN model demonstrates consistent restoration performance against the
input degraded images, with full training curves provided in the Appendices
and a summary of results in Table~\ref{tab:dncnn_results_summary}.
For the blur-only variants, the model achieved average PSNR values of
32.90, 32.76, and 32.66 dB for blur $\sigma$ of 5, 10, and 15 respectively,
compared to input PSNR values of 25.94, 25.85, and 25.83 dB. Performance
degraded progressively with increasing noise severity: variants with
noise $\sigma$ of 15, 20, and 25 yielded output PSNR values of 29.45, 28.11, and
26.61 dB against input PSNR values of 24.86, 22.45, and 20.60 dB respectively.
The mixed degradation variant, combining blur $\sigma$ of 10 with noise $\sigma$
of 15, proved the most challenging, with the model recovering to 24.73 dB from
an input of 22.07 dB. Across all variants the model yields a consistent PSNR
improvement of approximately 6--7 dB for blur-dominated degradations and 3--6 dB
for noise-dominated ones, suggesting stronger generalisation to blur than to
high-variance noise.

Following the cross-validation experiments across all seven dataset variants, a
final model was trained on the complete sample set of the mixed degradation variant
— combining blur $\sigma$ of 10 and noise $\sigma$ of 15 — representing the most
challenging and realistic degradation condition. The data was split into an 80\%
training set and a 20\% validation set, with the reported PSNR measured on the
held-out validation split. The resulting model achieved a PSNR of 28.98 dB, an
improvement over the cross-validation estimate of 24.73 dB, attributable to the
increased volume of training data afforded by training on the full dataset.
Per-epoch validation curves against the dataset baseline are provided in
Figure~\ref{fig:ds6_dncnn_final} of the Appendices.

\subsection{Uformer}

Uformer achieved a PSNR of 22.95 dB and an MSE of 0.0057 on this variant.
This is notably lower than its performance would likely be on blur-only
conditions, where PSNR values across the dataset range from approximately
25.83 to 25.94 dB, but is comparable to the noise-only variant at
$\sigma_n = 20$ (PSNR of 22.45 dB, MSE of 0.0057), suggesting that the
additive noise component of the mixed degradation contributes
substantially more to the difficulty of the task than the blur component.
Given that Uformer was pretrained on GoPro, a dataset oriented towards
motion deblurring rather than denoising, this pattern is consistent with
the model being comparatively better suited to handling blur artefacts
than noise, with performance degrading once a significant noise component
is introduced.

\subsection{Restormer}

Restormer achieved a PSNR of 26.72 dB and an MSE of 0.0033 on the
mixed-degradation variant, outperforming Uformer by a considerable margin
and approaching the PSNR levels observed on the blur-only variants
($\sigma_b \in \{5, 10, 15\}$), which range from 25.83 to 25.94 dB. This
result also exceeds performance on all three noise-only variants,
including the comparatively mild $\sigma_n = 15$ case (PSNR of 24.86 dB),
indicating that Restormer handles the compound degradation more
effectively than either degradation type might suggest in isolation. This
is consistent with Restormer having been pretrained on SIDD, a dataset
specifically constructed for real-world denoising, suggesting that its
denoising-oriented pretraining transfers well even when blur is
introduced alongside noise.
